# Developers Cannot Report It Either

The first essay closed one door. If the dashboards do not measure technical debt, the natural next move is to ask the people who wrote the code. They were there, so they naturally know what shortcuts were taken, where the model is partial, which modules were rushed. The reflex is reasonable. It is also, as a stable epistemic strategy, broken in ways that have nothing to do with the developers themselves.

This essay shows why. It does not argue that developers are unreliable witnesses to their own work in some general sense; that would be a different and unpleasant claim. It argues that the specific question *where is the debt in this codebase*, addressed to those who wrote it, is structurally answered with distortion. The distortion is not malice or incompetence. It is built into the conditions under which code gets written, remembered, and discussed.

The argument matters because the institutional fallback, when measurement fails, is to convene the developers and ask them. Workshops are run. Surveys are circulated. Tech leads hold sessions. Each developer is asked to identify the indebted regions of the system. The lists are compared, sometimes synthesized, often presented to management as the team’s view of its own debt. The procedure feels rigorous. The output is treated as ground truth. What follows in this essay is why that output should be treated more carefully than that.

## Why the reflex is reasonable

In most epistemic situations, asking the people closest to the work is the right move. Doctors ask patients about their symptoms. Investigators interview witnesses. Anthropologists rely on informants. The intuition that situated knowledge cannot be replaced by external instruments is correct in general, and it has a long tradition in the social sciences and in software engineering itself. The literature on tacit knowledge, on craft expertise, on the value of insider testimony, is large and largely correct.

So when a manager faces a codebase whose dashboards have failed her and turns to the developers, she is doing what good epistemics suggests. She is right to suspect that the team knows things the tools do not. The team does. The question is whether the team’s knowledge of *technical debt specifically* has the form that workshops and surveys assume it has, namely a list of regions that can be pointed to, named, and ranked. The answer, for three independent reasons, is that it does not, or at least not without distortions that the procedure cannot correct.

The three reasons compose. Each can be objected to in isolation. Their conjunction forms a condition that no individual correction can lift. The argument below sets them out one at a time, then shows why their composition is the relevant level.

## The epistemic mechanism

Cunningham’s original formulation, as the first essay noted, defines debt as the gap between the form shipped and the developer’s *current* understanding of the domain. The current understanding is itself partial. That is what makes the debt possible. The developer who shipped the code did so knowing the model was approximate; the approximation was conscious; the intention was to revise once production revealed what the model was missing.

A consequence follows that is rarely stated. At the moment of writing, the developer does not know *the form of their own ignorance*. They know they are taking a shortcut, but they do not know which aspects of their model will turn out to be wrong, which will turn out to be right by accident, which will be exposed by future requirements they have not yet imagined. They can name some shortcuts (the ones they took deliberately) but not the misunderstandings (the ones they took without seeing them). Six months later, when asked where the debt lies, they will report the deliberate shortcuts. They will not report the misunderstandings, because they still cannot see them. The misunderstandings are visible only in retrospect, after the next change has revealed what the original model could not handle.

This is not a failure of competence. It is a limit of reflective access: competence does not entail transparent access to the conditions under which one’s own model will later fail. An expert can point to where they cut corners; they cannot point to where they were simply wrong, because if they could see their wrongness they would have written differently. The debt that the developer can name is the debt that was contracted *as such*. The debt that weighs the most is often the debt that was contracted unknowingly, by a model that seemed adequate at the time and turned out, much later, not to be.

Time makes this worse. The shortcuts that were vivid at the moment of writing fade. Six months later, the code has been read again, integrated into the team’s working memory, normalized by the conventions of the codebase. It has become *what we do here*. The same developer who could have flagged ten regions as indebted at the moment of shipping will, a year later, flag two or three. Not because the debt has been paid down (it usually has not) but because the awareness of debt is itself perishable. The ones who could best report what they once knew have lost the access. The ones who can still see the debt have started rationalizing it as design.

This means that developer reports have a quite specific shape. They are relatively strong for *deliberate, recently contracted, locally confined* debt: shortcuts the developer remembers taking, in code they still inhabit. They are weak for *inadvertent* debt (the kind that comes from a model that turned out to be wrong), for *normalized* debt (the kind that has been absorbed into the team’s habits), for *distributed* debt (the kind that lives across multiple modules and is therefore not anyone’s to flag), and for *future-facing* debt (the kind whose cost depends on changes not yet required). The first kind is reportable. The other four are less reliably reportable, and they often become visible only when later change gives them a shape.

## The cognitive mechanism

The literature on introspection, beginning with Nisbett and Wilson in 1977 and refined by decades of subsequent work in metacognition, establishes a finding that is uncomfortable but well supported: people report their own mental processes poorly, and they often confabulate explanations after the fact. This finding does not mean that introspection is worthless; it means that introspective reports are *constructed*, not *retrieved*, and that the construction is biased in predictable ways.

When a developer is asked *where is the debt in your codebase*, the answer they give is constructed in the same way. It is not retrieved from a stored map. It is assembled in the moment, drawing on whatever happens to be available to memory at the time of asking. What is available is heavily biased by recency. The module that caused friction last week is salient. The module that has been smoothly running for three years is invisible, even if it harbors the deeper structural problems. The list of indebted regions reported by a developer correlates strongly with the list of regions that have caused them pain in the last quarter, and only loosely with the list of regions that *will* cause pain in the next quarter, which is the list the manager wanted.

A second cognitive limit compounds this. Nobody holds the entire system in memory. A senior developer who has worked on a codebase for five years has dense knowledge of three or four modules, sparser knowledge of a dozen more, and almost no knowledge of the rest. Empirical work on developer cognition (LaToza, Venolia and DeLine, among others) shows that developers expend significant effort *reconstructing* their understanding of code each time they return to it, by reading, by exploring, by interrupting colleagues. The mental model is not stored as a stable map; it is rebuilt under cost, partially, each time. A developer’s report on *the debt in the codebase* is, structurally, a report on the debt in the regions whose model they have recently rebuilt. The regions they have not touched are absent from their map, not because those regions are debt-free but because their cognitive access to those regions has decayed. Aggregating the reports of multiple developers can mitigate this somewhat, but it cannot fix it, because the modules that nobody currently inhabits (often the most quietly indebted) remain absent from every report.

A third effect, subtler than the other two, is *reconstruction under interrogation*. When a developer is asked to justify why they think a region is indebted, they will produce reasons. Some of these reasons will be the actual cause of the judgment; others will be post-hoc rationalization. Distinguishing the two is not possible from the outside, and often not possible from the inside either. The output of a debt workshop is a set of reasoned-sounding lists, but the reasoning has been generated to fit a judgment that was originally intuitive, and the intuition was itself biased by the recency effect described above. The reasoning gives the output an air of rigor that the underlying signal does not warrant.

## The sociological mechanism

The first two mechanisms operate even in a vacuum, with a single developer reporting in good faith. The third operates in the social context where reporting actually happens, and it is the most consequential.

Declaring debt is a costly act in an organization. The cost has several components. The most obvious is time: writing a TODO with proper context, raising a backlog item, drafting a retrospective note. None of this is captured by the metrics under which developers are evaluated (velocity, story points, tickets closed). The developer who reports debt thoroughly is, at the margin, working against their own measured performance.

The cost has a less obvious dimension that is sociological rather than economic. Declaring debt is *politically charged*. It can be read as an admission of personal weakness (*I should have done better*), as an implicit critique of management pressure (*you forced me to ship too fast*), or as an indictment of a colleague (*whoever wrote this was careless*). None of these readings is neutral. The developer who reports debt is taking a position in a small political field, whether they want to or not, and the position is rarely advantageous. So the rational developer, in many organizations, *underreports*.

The pressure can also push the other way, and this is where the picture gets more complicated. In some teams, declaring debt is valued as a sign of technical maturity. Acknowledging shortcuts becomes a marker of seniority. Saying *this module is not great, we know it, here is what we would do better* signals that the speaker has the perspective to see beyond the immediate. In such cultures, the developer who fails to flag enough debt is suspected of lacking judgment. The same rational developer, then, often *overreports*. Every approximation becomes a TODO. Every non-canonical pattern becomes a regretful comment. The marker saturates and loses its information value, because everything is flagged.

These two regimes coexist across the industry, and any individual team is somewhere on a spectrum between them. The crucial point is that the manager who runs a debt workshop cannot read its output without first reading the reporting culture that produced it. If the team underreports, the workshop produces a sanitized list that misses most of the actual debt. If the team overreports, the workshop produces a saturated list that has lost discriminative power. In neither case is the output a reliable map of the debt; in both cases, the output is *a portrait of the team’s reporting culture*, dressed up as a portrait of the codebase.

This is not a flaw that better facilitation can fix. It is a property of how speech about one’s own work is shaped by the social conditions of that speech. The same developers, asked the same question in two different organizational contexts, will produce two different lists. Both lists will be sincere. Neither will be the debt.

The deeper point is that the three mechanisms select different absences. The epistemic mechanism hides what was not yet knowable. The cognitive mechanism hides what is no longer accessible. The sociological mechanism hides what is not safely sayable. A debt workshop does not merely receive imperfect reports. It receives reports already shaped by the intersection of the unknowable, the forgotten, and the unsayable.

## A note on cross-reporting

A natural fallback, when self-reporting fails, is to ask developers about *each other’s* code. This seems to bypass the most obvious biases (the author of a module is invested in their own work; an outsider can see it more clearly). The fallback is real but limited, and the limits are important.

A developer who did not write the code reads its *artifactuality* without access to the *intentional history* that produced it. They see the layer; they do not see the chain of constraints, bugs, library quirks, and anticipated requirements that made the layer the way it is. They will overdiagnose where they see patterns of debt that resemble what they have seen elsewhere, and underdiagnose where the debt is unfamiliar in shape. They will project their own coding preferences onto the work, which sometimes catches real debt and sometimes catches taste differences that are not debt at all.

Cross-reporting is also more politically charged than self-reporting, not less. To name a colleague’s code as indebted is an act with consequences (territorial, reputational, sometimes professional) that few developers will undertake casually. The lucid observer, in most organizational contexts, *underreports what they see in others*, for the same reasons of organizational survival that operate in the self-reporting case. The cross-reports that do circulate are often not the most accurate diagnoses but the most politically defensible ones, which is again a different signal than the one the manager wanted.

## What is admitted, and what is not

A reader familiar with the empirical literature will object at this point that some debt *is* directly reported by developers, and that this body of evidence cannot be set aside. The objection is correct, and it deserves a precise response.

There is a research tradition, beginning with Potdar and Shihab, on what is called *self-admitted technical debt*: the debt that developers explicitly mark in code, through comments such as TODO, FIXME, HACK, *temporary*, *workaround*, and similar inscriptions. Subsequent work (Maldonado, Shihab, and others) has refined the typology, distinguishing self-admitted debt of design, defect, documentation, requirement, and test. The phenomenon is real, the literature is rigorous, and the markers do carry information. A codebase rich in such markers reveals something a codebase without them does not.

The question is what those markers reveal. They reveal the subset of debt that was, at the moment of writing, *visible to the author*, *nameable in a comment*, and *socially admissible* under the team’s reporting culture. They reveal what the author was both able and willing to inscribe. They do not reveal the debt the author could not see (the misunderstandings of the epistemic mechanism), the debt the author had forgotten (the perishable awareness of the cognitive mechanism), or the debt the author found politically risky to inscribe (the sociological mechanism). Self-admitted debt is therefore not an escape from the problem this essay is describing. It is one more signal inside it: valuable, situated, partial, and shaped by the same conditions that govern all developer speech about code.

The literature itself supports a cautious reading. Self-admitted debt markers are informative, but their relationship to later quality outcomes is heterogeneous. Some marked regions resolve quietly. Some unmarked regions become acute problems. The markers are *one input* to the team’s evolving knowledge of its codebase, alongside change history, friction, and the other signals discussed above. They do not, on their own, constitute that knowledge. Treating them as if they did would be the same category error the first essay identified for dashboards, repeated at the level of developer testimony.

## What knowledge of debt actually looks like

The picture that emerges is severe. Debt is not contained by the code, the first essay argued. Debt is not contained by developer reports either, this essay has argued. The natural conclusion, that nothing can be known about debt, is wrong, but the right conclusion takes a moment to set out.

The right conclusion is that knowledge of debt is *triangulated and longitudinal*, never primary and synchronic. No single source has direct access. What is available, instead, is a set of partial signals, each of which is unreliable on its own but which can be cross-checked against each other over time. The structural signatures that the code exposes (what the dashboards detect, read as locations rather than as quantities). The friction observed when change passes through (rework rates, review cycles, rollback frequency). The self-admissions developers do inscribe (read as one signal among others, not as ground truth). The change program of the organization (which determines where latent debt will become costly). The historical ruptures (post-mortems, missed deadlines, abandoned features) that crystallize, in retrospect, where the debt was concentrated.

None of these is the debt. None of them, taken alone, can be trusted. But a team that watches them over time, that knows its own history, that can correlate signals across sources and across years, develops something that resembles knowledge. The knowledge is not a measurement. It is a *narrative under revision*: an evolving account of where the debt lives, what it has cost so far, what changes are likely to expose it next. This narrative is owned by the team, distributed across its members, partially tacit, and not reducible to a document. It can be made more rigorous, but it cannot be replaced by a single instrument or a single procedure.

The implication for organizational practice is uncomfortable. There is no clean handoff between the developers and management. There is no procedure that takes the team’s knowledge and converts it into a report that an outsider can read and act on. The knowledge is genuinely held by the people who hold it, and extracting it requires a relationship with them that is patient, longitudinal, and not adversarial. Workshops and surveys can contribute inputs, but they cannot produce the knowledge they are supposed to extract, because the knowledge is not in the heads of the developers in a form that workshops and surveys can pull out.

## Why this matters for what follows

This essay closes the second of the doors that the first essay opened. Measurement does not give primary access, and self-report does not give it either. Both failures are structural. Neither is a defect that better tools or better methods can fix.

What this leaves is a more difficult position to hold, and the rest of this folder is written from that position. If debt cannot be measured and cannot be directly reported, then the very category of debt, as it functions in industry conversations, is suspect. It is treated as if it were an object that the right instrument could detect. It is, instead, a relation that becomes visible only at the moment of friction and that no source can access in advance.

This shift makes possible a question the next essay takes up. The category of *technical debt* is usually distinguished from *over-engineering*. Debt is what was undershipped (too little form, too quickly). Over-engineering is what was overshipped (too much form, too speculatively). The distinction has felt natural, and it has organized a good deal of the industry’s thinking. But under the regime that begins to emerge here, the distinction may not survive. If debt becomes visible only at the moment of friction, then over-engineering may turn out to be debt that has not yet had its moment, debt whose trial has not yet come. The next essay tests this hypothesis, and what it finds reorganizes the map.

For now, the working position is this. The first essay argued that debt is not contained by the code. This essay has argued that debt is not contained by developer reports either. What is in the code is substrate. What is in developer reports is partial, perishable, and socially shaped testimony. What is in the team, over time, is a narrative that approximates the debt without ever capturing it. The discipline of working with debt is the discipline of holding that narrative honestly, accepting that no shortcut to it exists.
