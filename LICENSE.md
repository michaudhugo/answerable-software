## Which license applies to what

If you are quoting an essay, reproducing a diagram, translating a passage, adapting a diagnostic framework, building a derived theoretical work, or reusing glossary text, you are using prose or conceptual material. CC BY-SA 4.0 applies.

If you are copying a code snippet into your own project, adapting it, or using it as a starting point for your own implementation, you are using code. The MIT License applies.

If a file contains both prose and code, the prose is licensed under CC BY-SA 4.0 and the code is licensed under the MIT License.

If you are unsure, the basic rule is simple: prose is CC BY-SA 4.0, code is MIT.

## How to cite this corpus

When citing the corpus as a whole in academic or professional work:

> Hugo Michaud. *answerable-software: On software systems that localize what they make impossible, what they commit to, and where their consequences return.* 2026. https://github.com/michaudhugo/answerable-software

When citing a specific essay, include the essay path and the version date you read. Each essay carries its own date in its header. The date matters because this corpus is revised over time, and citations should be anchored to a specific version.

When citing a concept, the canonical reference is its entry in [`GLOSSARY.md`](./GLOSSARY.md), which links back to the essay where the concept was first developed.

## Why this license

A theoretical corpus has two enemies.

The first is silence: being unread, unused, and untransmitted.

The second is appropriation: being copied, adapted, or repackaged without attribution, or enclosed under terms that prevent others from extending what was originally open.

CC BY-SA 4.0 is chosen to address both.

It addresses silence by allowing broad transmission. You can quote, translate, teach from this material, build on it, present it, distribute it, and use it commercially, provided the license terms are respected. The work is meant to travel.

It addresses appropriation by requiring attribution and openness of adapted material. The author's name cannot be erased from copied or adapted material. The openness of adapted material cannot be closed. A consultant cannot copy or adapt substantial parts of these essays, repackage them, and sell the result as a proprietary methodology without respecting CC BY-SA 4.0. A publisher cannot extract substantial protected material from this corpus and place the adapted result behind terms that prohibit the freedoms granted by this license. A platform cannot copy, adapt, or embed substantial licensed material from this corpus into a closed product without respecting the CC BY-SA 4.0 terms.

The deal is simple. Take what is here. Use it. Build on it. Teach it. Translate it. Disagree with it in print. Reference it in your own corpus. The requirements are that you credit the source, indicate changes where required, and keep adapted material under the same open terms.

This is what makes a corpus answerable for itself: it can be carried forward, but it cannot be quietly enclosed.
